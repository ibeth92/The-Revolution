    Music genre is defined as “a conventional category that identifies some pieces of music as belonging to a shared tradition or set of conventions.” As with all other forms of artistic expression, music genre is subject to interpretation and there is a lot of overlap. Some common examples of music genre are: pop, classical, rock, and reggae. Some lesser-known music genres (according to Spotify): animal singing, drone metal, vegan straight edge, and shimmer psych.
    Technology has changed the way music is composed, heard, and transmitted. For example, “Strawberry Fields Forever” is said to have been 2 different recorded versions that were spliced together and adjusted for tempo and pitch, therefore creating the song we know today. Google’s Magenta Project can produce songs that have been written and are performed by AI. Technology is becoming more and more involved in the creative process. LANDR, an AI-based mastering service, allows artists to master their own tracks by replicating human engineers with AI and machine learning and they have helped 2 million musicians master more than 10 million songs.
    Yes and no. With technology being used more often in the creation and production processes, certain music features that may sound like they belong to different genres, can actually be more closely related when extracted and compared. However, there are specific features that distinguish one genre from another. For example, classical music refers to most orchestral styles with no vocals, whereas metal features a band setup with electric guitars, bass, and drums, and a 'heavy' and aggressive sound.

Spectrograms

A spectrogram is a visual representation of the spectrum of frequencies of a signal as it varies with time.

Each graph is made of two geometric dimensions - one axis represents time, the other represents frequency. A third dimension indicates the amplitude of a particular frequency and is represented by a change in color. The higher the intensity, the brighter the color.
