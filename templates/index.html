“The only truth is music.”

― Jack Kerouac

    Music genre is defined as “a conventional category that identifies some pieces of music as belonging to a shared tradition or set of conventions.” As with all other forms of artistic expression, music genre is subject to interpretation and there is a lot of overlap. Some common examples of music genre are: pop, classical, rock, and reggae. Some lesser-known music genres (according to Spotify): animal singing, drone metal, vegan straight edge, and shimmer psych.
    Technology has changed the way music is composed, heard, and transmitted. For example, “Strawberry Fields Forever” is said to have been 2 separate versions that were spliced together and adjusted for tempo and pitch. Google’s Magenta Project can produce songs that have been written and are performed by AI. Technology is becoming more and more involved in the creative process. LANDR, an AI-based mastering service, allows artists to master their own tracks by replicating human engineers with AI and machine learning and they have helped 2 million musicians master more than 10 million songs.
    It’s hard to say. Every creative process is born from the influences of what someone else has already done. Painters can be influenced by Monet and Pollock, writers by Hemingway and King. The same goes for musicians. The days of listening to or creating one style of music are over. If you were to look at someone’s list of downloaded music you would find a wide range of styles, tempos, and moods. So wouldn’t it make sense for a musician to take a little piece of this and a pinch of that to create something that doesn’t fit some predefined mold.

Jazz has borrowed from other genres of music and also has lent itself to other genres of music.

- Herbie Hancock
